                          Matter-Antimatter Duality

The first strong evidence for the existence of antimatter appeared in 1932, when
physicist Carl Anderson discovered the positron in a cloud chamber. He observed a
particle that behaved like an electron in a magnetic field but curved in the opposite
direction, which meant it had the same amount of mass but an opposite electric
charge.
    This discovery was a direct confirmation of the theoretical predictions of quan-
tum physics, specifically the Dirac equation, which had predicted the existence of
antimatter a few years earlier. Later evidence became even stronger. In modern
laboratories and particle accelerators, such as those at CERN, antimatter is regularly
produced. Collisions of particles at high energies create antiprotons, positrons, and
other antiparticles, which are then detected, measured, and analyzed using extremely
precise instruments.
    Scientists have even succeeded in combining an antiproton and a positron into
antihydrogen, that is, an anti-atom of hydrogen. Such anti-atoms are temporarily
trapped in magnetic traps, and by measuring the light they absorb and emit, it has
been confirmed that they behave almost identically to ordinary hydrogen, except for
the sign of the charge.
    One of the most certain proofs of the existence of antimatter is the characteristic
gamma radiation that occurs during annihilation. For example, when an electron
and a positron are destroyed, radiation of a precisely determined energy (511 keV)
is produced, which can be accurately measured. Such a signal has no other possible
explanation other than the presence of antimatter.
    Antimatter is not used only in laboratories but has practical applications in
medicine as well. Positron Emission Tomography (PET scanners), which are
used daily for the diagnosis of tumors and other diseases, are based precisely on the
annihilation of positrons and electrons. If antimatter did not exist, this technology
would not be possible at all.
    Additional evidence comes to us from space. Antiparticles, such as positrons
and antiprotons, have been detected in cosmic rays and in extreme astrophysical
environments, such as the regions around pulsars and black holes. Satellites and
space detectors regularly record their arrival at Earth.

    Samir Džolota is an author and independent researcher based in Sarajevo, Bosnia
and Herzegovina, whose long-term work is dedicated to the theoretical investigation
of energy, fundamental interactions, and the structural principles that form the
basis of physical reality. Operating outside of formal academic institutions, Džolota
develops original conceptual models that seek to reinterpret established physical
phenomena through the prism of continuous energy exchange. His research focus
encompasses fundamental questions of physics, including the nature of free energy,
quantum entanglement, gravity, temperature, and time. The central thesis of his work,
formalized through the Unified Energy Exchange Theory (UEET), rests on the idea that
many physical quantities are not fundamental constants, but emergent phenomena
resulting from dynamic processes of energy exchange between matter and its physical
environment.
    Džolota is the author of several theoretical works that together constitute an evolu-
tionary attempt at conceptual unification in physics. In his book Hidden Secret of Free
Energy, he examines free energy from theoretical and thermodynamic perspectives,
emphasizing flow mechanisms and system dynamics. The work Teslion and Quantum
Entanglement explores non-local quantum phenomena and their possible connections
to energy transfer, drawing inspiration from the ideas of Nikola Tesla. In the work
Pyramid Portals, the author explores the hypothesis of pyramidal structures as energy
modulation systems, linking geometry and resonance with field interactions. His
work Theory of Everything – Ordered Chaos represents a profound synthesis in which
apparent physical chaos is interpreted as a manifestation of a fundamental energetic
order.
    His latest work, Unified Energy Exchange: Predictive Theory of Everything, represents
the pinnacle of this research journey, offering a unified theoretical framework that
interprets physical reality as an emergent consequence of oscillatory energy exchange
between dual fundamental states. Within this model, matter, space, time, and forces
of interaction arise from dynamic imbalances in energy distribution, rather than
being treated as independent entities. The author integrates concepts of quantum
mechanics, relativity, and cosmology, offering an ontological reinterpretation of known
phenomena that remains consistent with experimental observations. Džolota’s work
is characterized by analytical rigor and a quest for internal consistency, providing a
coherent alternative perspective on the very nature of reality.
